02 — Philosophy

Why teams exist at all.

Specialization is not fragmentation. It is how systems achieve depth that generalism never can.

Most clubs divide members into committees. Athenaeum divides responsibility into engineering layers. The distinction matters more than it sounds.

A committee exists to complete tasks. A division exists to accumulate expertise over time. Members who join the Systems team do not dabble in systems — they build intuition for low-level architecture, concurrency, and infrastructure across every semester they are here.

Depth in one area always beats surface-level exposure to many.

Athenaeum's product goal is collective: five specialist teams build one full-stack, cross-platform application together. Each team owns a layer. All layers must integrate. This is how real software companies operate.

You do not move between teams randomly. You build mastery in one place — and then, when your depth is sufficient, you teach others. That obligation to teach is how the whole system compounds.

Ownership Model

Each team owns its layer end-to-end. No one else holds your architecture accountable.

Systems Thinking

Your decisions affect every other team. Build with integration in mind, always.

Teaching Obligation

Mastering a skill carries a responsibility. Share its foundations with every junior member.

Long-Term Depth

One semester of focused work beats three semesters of scattered exposure.

03 — Divisions

The five specialist teams.

Each division owns a distinct layer of the engineering stack. Together they ship one product.

SYS
Division · 01

Systems Team

The foundation that every other team builds on. The Systems team is responsible for infrastructure architecture, build tooling, deployment pipelines, and the low-level engineering decisions that determine whether the entire product holds under pressure.

Members work at the intersection of operating systems, concurrency, process management, and real deployment environments. This is not DevOps as a service — this is engineering ownership of the stack itself.

Engineering Focus

Linux internals · process scheduling · memory architecture · CI/CD pipelines · containerisation · system design at scale · build tooling · cross-platform compatibility

Rust Go Linux Docker Git Shell System Design CI/CD
DB
Division · 02

Database Team

Data architecture is not configuration — it is engineering. The DB team designs the schemas, owns the query layer, and makes the decisions that determine whether the product's data model scales and stays correct.

Engineering Focus

Normalisation, indexing strategies, query plan analysis, transaction semantics, and the deep understanding of why databases behave the way they do.

SQL SurrealDB PostgreSQL Query Planning Schema Design
CRE
Division · 03

Creative Team

Visual communication is a technical discipline. The Creative team engineers interfaces, builds cross-platform frontends, and takes responsibility for the product experience that end users actually encounter.

Engineering Focus

Component architecture, state management, accessibility, performance budgets, and building UI systems that remain maintainable as the codebase scales.

React Native Flutter TypeScript UI Systems Accessibility
CYB
Division · 04

Cyber Team

Security is not a feature you add later. The Cyber team embeds threat modelling into every layer of the product — identifying attack surfaces, analysing vulnerabilities, and ensuring the product holds against adversarial conditions.

Engineering Focus

Network security, authentication architecture, vulnerability analysis, penetration testing methodology, and the deep study of how systems fail under attack.

Networking Security Tooling Linux Threat Modelling CTF
MKT
Division · 05

Marketing Team

Technical work has no value if it cannot be communicated. The Marketing team handles outreach, documentation, and the external representation of Athenaeum's engineering — translating real depth into language that reaches the right people.

Engineering Focus

Technical writing, audience research, structured communication strategy, and building the feedback loops that tell engineering teams what the outside world actually needs.

Technical Writing Documentation Git Communication
04 — Integration

How the divisions connect.

Five teams. One product. Every architectural decision crosses team boundaries.

01 · Systems

Infrastructure

Build environment, deployment pipeline, and the system architecture that every other layer depends on.

02 · Database

Data Layer

Schema design, query architecture, and the data contracts that the application and API layers consume.

03 · Creative

Interface

Cross-platform frontend that consumes the API and translates data architecture into user-facing behaviour.

04 · Cyber

Security Layer

Threat modelling and vulnerability analysis embedded across every layer — not applied as an afterthought.

05 · Marketing

Communication

Technical documentation, outreach strategy, and external representation of what the other four teams build.

Every team's output becomes another team's constraint. This is not a problem to manage — it is the engineering discipline we are here to develop. Systems that must integrate with other systems build better engineers than systems built in isolation.

05 — Progression

How members evolve inside a team.

Progression is evidenced, not declared. You move up when your output demonstrates it.

Stage I Foundation Builder

Contributor

You are learning the domain from first principles. You complete defined tasks, ask structured questions, and build the foundational intuition your team's work requires.

Daily practice. Re-learn one concept per week from scratch. Build small, working tools. Learn Git hygiene from commit one.

Evidence required: 30+ solved problems · one deployed CLI tool · can explain one core subject without notes
Stage II Skill Builder

Active Builder

You code independently, own small subsystems, and have shipped your first real project. You are beginning to go meaningfully beyond what class teaches.

Leetcode Medium consistently. Read primary source documentation. Build first real project — not a tutorial clone. Make your first open source contribution.

Evidence required: real GitHub project with 10+ commits · taught something to the club · consistent Medium-level problem solving
Stage III Depth Seeker

Specialist

You have a deployed project. You read documentation naturally. You are beginning to reason about system design and have opinions grounded in real experience.

System design fundamentals. Codeforces Div2 competitive programming. Meaningful open source contributions. Read CS research papers. Apply to internships.

Evidence required: multiple strong projects · regularly helps others debug · reads advanced resources independently
Stage IV Technical Lead

Team Lead & Mentor

You can teach any core topic in your domain. You run teach-back sessions and code reviews. Your impact is measured by how much the people around you have grown.

Run structured learning sessions. Review other members' code with real feedback. Read advanced resources and bring what you find back to the team.

Evidence required: active mentor · multiple real projects · regularly leads discussions · applied to internships
06 — Practice

What real engineering means here.

Teams do not simulate work. They do the work — with all the difficulty that implies.

01 · OWNERSHIP

You own the problem.

Not a task assigned by a manager. Your team owns its layer — the architecture decisions, the bugs, the refactors, and the teaching of what you learned through the failures.

02 · REAL PROJECTS

Built from scratch.

Every project must be explainable line by line. If you cannot explain why you used that library, why the schema looks like that, or what the bottleneck is — it is not a real project yet.

03 · DEPTH

One layer, deeply.

Surface-level exposure to many things is the syllabus. Engineering depth is what happens when you spend a semester going three levels deeper into one domain than class ever required.

04 · ITERATION

Build, break, rebuild.

Real engineering is not one clean implementation. It is ten iterations, three rearchitectures, and the accumulated judgement about what not to do that you only earn by doing it wrong first.

05 · DISCIPLINE

Standards, always.

Meaningful commit messages. Clean README. Functions that do one thing. Names that explain themselves. These are not stylistic preferences — they are the minimum standard of professional engineering.

06 · COLLABORATION

Integrate, don't silo.

Your technical decisions constrain other teams. The discipline of building with integration in mind — communicating contracts, documenting interfaces — is the skill that distinguishes engineers from individual contributors.
